## 1. Why rules are needed

AI tools accelerate routine work, but they cannot own a research
contribution. Without explicit rules, the boundary between "AI
helped me" and "AI did my work" blurs. The six rules below are the
group's working contract: they enable productive AI use while
preserving the researcher's ownership and the group's integrity.

The rules are not preferences. Violations risk academic
misconduct findings, paper retractions, and career consequences.

## 2. Rule 1: Permitted AI uses

Allowed uses of AI tools, including LLMs:

- Literature search and organisation: retrieving papers,
  summarising related work for the user's own reading, managing
  bibliography files.
- Code support and debugging: generating scaffolding, explaining
  compiler errors, proposing refactors.
- Language and expression polish: grammar correction, phrasing
  suggestions, paragraph restructuring for flow.

All of these are mechanical acceleration. None of them replace
the user's scientific judgment.

## 3. Rule 2: Owned by the user

The following must be the user's own work and must be fully
understood by the user:

- Research ideas.
- Research problems and framings.
- Research design.
- Technical pathways and algorithmic choices.
- Experimental plans.
- Core conclusions.
- Novelty claims.

AI can ask clarifying questions; AI can point out overlooked
options; AI cannot make these decisions. If the user cannot
explain a design choice without referring to what AI said, the
user does not own it.

## 4. Rule 3: Verify every AI output

Every AI-generated or AI-assisted passage requires user
verification:

- Code must be read, understood, and tested.
- Prose must be cross-checked against the research process and
  experimental results; no discrepancies or exaggerations.
- Numerical claims must be traced back to experiment outputs.

Verification is sentence-by-sentence for prose and line-by-line
for code. Spot-checking is insufficient.

## 5. Rule 4: No fabricated citations

Citations must come from the user's own literature search,
reading, and confirmation. AI must never:

- Invent paper titles, authors, or venues.
- Generate BibTeX entries for papers that do not exist.
- Cite a paper the user has not read.

When AI proposes a citation, the user must:

1. Find the paper in a scholarly source (Google Scholar, DBLP,
   arXiv).
2. Read at least the abstract.
3. Confirm the claim the user is making matches the paper's
   actual content.

If any of these fail, the citation is removed.

## 6. Rule 5: No academic misconduct

Never use AI to:

- Generate fake data or experimental results.
- Fabricate, alter, or invent experimental procedures.
- Hide or paraphrase plagiarism from prior work.
- Rewrite another group's results as the user's own.

Academic misconduct, whether AI-assisted or not, is a career-
ending line. AI-assisted misconduct is not less serious; if
anything, it is easier to detect (AI-generated fake data often
has statistical signatures).

## 7. Rule 6: Honour venue disclosure rules

Many conferences, journals, and schools have explicit AI
disclosure or usage rules for paper submissions and theses. These
rules are evolving and vary widely:

- Some require disclosure of AI-assisted drafting.
- Some forbid AI-generated content in specific sections.
- Some require attestation that the author wrote the submitted
  text.

The user's responsibility:

- Check the target venue's current policy before submission.
- Consult the advisor when in doubt.
- Disclose AI usage in the paper or submission form as
  required.
- Comply with institutional policies as well as venue policies.

## 8. Compliance examples

- "I used Cursor to scaffold the training loop; I wrote all the
  loss functions and verified each gradient by hand." Compliant.
- "Claude suggested three ways to structure the Introduction; I
  picked one, rewrote it in my own words, and confirmed it
  matches my actual contribution." Compliant.
- "ChatGPT corrected grammar in my Abstract; I read the changes
  line by line and re-verified that each claim is in my paper."
  Compliant.

## 9. Non-compliance examples

- "I asked Claude to write the entire Related Work section and
  inserted the output." Non-compliant (rule 2, 3).
- "ChatGPT gave me five citations that looked plausible; I added
  them to my bibliography." Non-compliant (rule 4).
- "I let the AI generate missing data points for the figure
  because the experiment did not quite work." Non-compliant
  (rule 5; this is a career-ending violation).
- "The submission form asks whether I used AI; I said no because
  I only used it for grammar." Non-compliant (rule 6; disclose
  honestly or follow the venue's definition).
- "My advisor said I should write everything myself; I used AI
  only for the final polish without telling them." Non-compliant
  (rule 2, 6).
